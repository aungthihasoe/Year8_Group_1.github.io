Earthquake Information

Learn what earthquakes are, how they affect people, and what families, schools, and neighbours can do before, during, and after shaking.

Read Safety Tips Emergency Kit

Quick Definition

An earthquake is ground shaking caused by sudden movement beneath the Earth's surface. The energy moves as seismic waves and can damage buildings, roads, and utilities.

We can't predict exact times for earthquakes — preparation is the best option.

Safety Tips

Before an Earthquake

  • Fix shelves and heavy furniture to the wall.
  • Keep shoes, a flashlight, water, and first aid easy to reach.
  • Know safe spots in each room, like under a strong table.
  • Practice drills at school and at home.

During an Earthquake

  • Drop, cover, and hold on.
  • Move away from windows, mirrors, and hanging objects.
  • If outside, go to an open space away from buildings and lines.
  • If in a car, stop safely away from bridges and trees.

After an Earthquake

  • Check yourself and others for injuries.
  • Leave damaged buildings carefully and avoid elevators.
  • Watch for aftershocks, broken glass, fires, and fallen wires.
  • Use text messages if phone networks are busy.

Community Safety

  • Mark safe open spaces and clear evacuation routes.
  • Help children, elderly, and disabled people during drills.
  • Share trusted emergency numbers with neighbours.
  • Keep community shelters organised and ready.

How Earthquakes Happen

1. Plates Move

Tectonic plates slowly push, pull, or slide past each other.

2. Pressure Builds

Rocks may get stuck, so pressure builds underground.

3. Rocks Slip

When pressure becomes too strong, rocks suddenly break or slip.

4. Ground Shakes

The released energy travels as waves and shakes the land.

Safer Everyday Preparation

Stronger Places

Check cracks, repair weak walls, fix heavy furniture, and improve important buildings like schools and clinics when possible.

Better Communication

Use family contact lists, school announcements, radios, and local community groups so people receive clear safety information quickly.

Reusable Supplies

Choose reusable bottles, solar lamps, rechargeable power banks, and repair items so supplies last longer and create less waste.

Magnitude and Damage

Magnitude measures earthquake energy, but damage also depends on how close people are, how strong buildings are, and whether the ground is soft or solid.

Magnitude Possible Effect
Below 3.0 Usually not felt by people.
3.0 - 4.9 May be felt, but often causes little damage.
5.0 - 6.9 Can damage weak buildings and objects inside homes.
7.0 and above Can cause serious damage over a large area.

Drop, Cover, Hold On

1

Drop

Get down on your hands and knees so the shaking does not knock you over.

2

Cover

Cover your head and neck under a strong desk or beside strong furniture.

3

Hold On

Hold your shelter until the shaking stops and it is safe to move.

Common Earthquake Dangers

Falling Objects

Bookshelves, glass, lights, and decorations can fall during shaking. Secure heavy objects before an earthquake.

Damaged Buildings

Cracks, weak walls, and broken stairs can make buildings unsafe. Leave carefully and wait for official advice.

Secondary Hazards

Earthquakes can cause fires, landslides, broken roads, and damaged water or electricity systems.
