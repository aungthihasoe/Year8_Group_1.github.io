Tornado Information

Learn what tornadoes are, how they form, warning signs to watch for, and how families, schools, and neighbours can prepare before, during, and after a tornado.

Read Safety Tips Emergency Kit

Quick Definition

A tornado is a fast-spinning column of air from a storm to the ground. It can cause severe damage to buildings, trees, and vehicles.

Tornadoes can change quickly — have a warning plan and a nearby shelter spot.

About Tornadoes

Tornadoes are dangerous wind storms that often form during strong thunderstorms. Some last only a few minutes, but their winds can throw objects, damage roofs, break windows, and make roads unsafe.

Strong Thunderstorms

Many tornadoes form when warm, moist air rises quickly and meets cooler air inside a severe thunderstorm.

Rotating Air

Winds at different heights may blow at different speeds or directions. This can create spinning air inside the storm.

Flying Debris

Flying debris is one of the biggest dangers. Glass, wood, metal, signs, and roof pieces can move very fast.

Important Tornado Words

These words help people understand weather reports, warnings, and safety instructions during severe storms.

A tornado watch means tornadoes are possible. A tornado warning means shelter should be taken immediately.
Word Meaning
Funnel Cloud A rotating cone-shaped cloud that has not reached the ground.
Tornado Watch Tornadoes are possible, so people should stay alert.
Tornado Warning A tornado has been seen or detected, so people should shelter quickly.
Debris Broken objects carried by strong tornado winds.
Safe Room A strong indoor room used for protection during dangerous weather.

How Tornadoes Happen

1. Warm Air Rises

Warm, moist air rises quickly inside a strong thunderstorm.

2. Winds Change

Winds at different heights move at different speeds or directions.

3. Air Rotates

The wind change can create a spinning column of air.

4. Funnel Touches Down

If the rotating air reaches the ground, it becomes a tornado.

Safer Everyday Preparation

Stronger Shelter

Choose a small interior room on the lowest floor. Keep it clear of clutter so family members can move there quickly.

Better Communication

Use local radio, school announcements, phone alerts, and family contact lists so warnings reach people fast.

Reusable Supplies

Prepare reusable water bottles, rechargeable lights, power banks, and repair items so supplies last longer and create less waste.

Tornado Strength

Tornado damage is often described using the Enhanced Fujita Scale, also called the EF Scale. It estimates wind strength by looking at damage.

Scale Possible Damage
EF0 Light damage, such as broken branches or minor roof damage.
EF1 Moderate damage, such as damaged roofs or moving small vehicles.
EF2 Considerable damage, such as torn roofs and broken large trees.
EF3 or higher Severe to extreme damage to strong buildings and large objects.

Safety Tips

Before a Tornado

  • Choose a small interior room on the lowest floor as your shelter.
  • Keep shoes, flashlight, first aid items, water, and power bank ready.
  • Charge phones when severe weather is expected.
  • Listen to trusted local weather alerts.

During a Tornado

  • Go to shelter immediately and stay low.
  • Stay away from windows, glass doors, and outside walls.
  • Cover your head and neck with your arms, bag, blanket, or pillow.
  • Do not go outside to watch or record the tornado.

After a Tornado

  • Check yourself and others for injuries.
  • Avoid broken glass, damaged buildings, and fallen power lines.
  • Use messages instead of phone calls if networks are busy.
  • Wait for official information before returning to damaged areas.

Community Safety

  • Keep shelter spaces clean and easy to enter.
  • Share warning information with neighbours who may not receive alerts.
  • Help children, elderly people, and disabled people reach shelter.
  • Keep roads clear for emergency workers after the storm.

Common Tornado Dangers

Flying Debris

Broken wood, glass, signs, and roof materials can fly very fast. Protecting your head and neck can reduce injury.

Falling Structures

Weak walls, roofs, and trees can fall during strong winds. Stay inside a strong shelter until the danger has passed.

Power Lines

Tornadoes can damage electric poles and wires. Never touch fallen wires or water near them.

Frequently Asked Questions
